What Defines a Kevin Willard Team?

2022-11-17

After spending more than ten seasons as Seton Hall’s head coach, Kevin Willard became Maryland’s new head man before the 2022-23 season. Willard had a successful tenure with the Pirates, reviving the program after a futile stretch under Bobby Gonzalez. Willard’s Seton Hall squads were known for their defense, so I wanted to explore just what defensive categories they especially excelled in under Willard. I did this by taking Seton Hall’s per-game averages from the 2011-12 through 2021-22 seasons and comparing them to Maryland’s – along with the rest of the NCAA’s averages during that time frame. I discovered that the Pirates were especially adept in perimeter defense, opponent field goal percentage and steals. But I went a little deeper to evaluate how Willard’s Seton Hall teams handled late-game pressure. I looked into how frequently Seton Hall played in games decided by three points or fewer and how frequently they won those games compared to other teams. Then I compared these figures to Maryland’s propensity to play in close games and how good it was at winning them. I found that Maryland fans can perhaps expect more nail-biting finishes, but it may not necessarily lead to a better overall winning percentage in one-possession contests.

Based on our class discussion, I got the impression that Seton Hall was known for its defense under Kevin Willard. So I examined Seton Hall’s per-game defensive statistics – like blocks, rebounds and steals – and checked where it ranked among all NCAA teams throughout the period between the 2011-12 and 2021-22 seasons. I found that Seton Hall ranked in the top 100 nationally in a handful of defensive categories, including opponent three-point percentage (42nd), opponent field goal percentage (68th) and steals (64th). I wanted to compare these defensive stats with Maryland’s during that same time, so I created separate dataframes showing just Maryland’s statistics, as well.

Next I was interested in 1) seeing how many close games Seton Hall played in, and 2) how well they did in those close games. I defined a 'close' game as one decided by three points or fewer. 
seton_hall_close_games <- seton_hall %>% 
  mutate(score_differential = TeamScore - OpponentScore)

seton_hall_close_games <- seton_hall_close_games %>% 
  filter(between(score_differential, -4, 4)) %>% 
  mutate(close_game_result = case_when(
    score_differential > 0 ~'W',
    score_differential < 0 ~ 'L'
  )) %>% 
mutate(close_game_win = case_when(
  score_differential > 0 ~ 1,
  score_differential < 0 ~ 0
))

I discovered that Seton Hall has played in 93 games decided by three points or fewer, which was tied for the 14th-most among all college basketball teams. From here, I was curious as to how frequently Seton Hall won these close games.

bbclosegames <- basketball_close_games %>% 
  group_by(TeamFull) %>%
  summarize(close_games=n()) %>% 
  arrange(desc(close_games))

bbclosegamewins <- basketball_close_games %>% 
  group_by(TeamFull) %>% 
  filter(close_game_win == 1) %>% 
  summarize(close_game_wins=n()) %>% 
  arrange(desc(close_game_wins))

inner_join(bbclosegames, bbclosegamewins, by = 'TeamFull') -> oneposgames

oneposwinpct <- oneposgames %>% 
  filter(close_games > 25) %>% 
  mutate(close_game_win_pct = (close_game_wins/close_games)*100) %>% 
  arrange(desc(close_game_win_pct))

From here I wanted to graph how frequently Seton Hall won games decided by three points or fewer and compare it to how frequently Maryland won games decided by three points or fewer during that same time. I create separate dataframes for both Seton Hall and Maryland to make sure they stood out on the scatterplot.

seton_hall_close_game_win_pct <- oneposwinpct %>%
